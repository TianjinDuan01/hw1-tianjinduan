# Why I Care About Healthcare and Clinical AI

I am a graduate student at Johns Hopkins University Carey Business School pursuing a Master of Science in Information Systems and Artificial Intelligence, with a background in Mathematics and Statistics from the University of Toronto. My experience spans data engineering, machine learning, and applied AI in both clinical and non-clinical settings. At Johns Hopkins, I have worked with EEG and ECG data, evaluated machine learning methods for diagnostic classification, and supported database systems for clinical variable retrieval. I have also worked on turning unstructured clinical notes into structured, analysis-ready data, which made healthcare data quality and usability feel like a real operational problem rather than just a technical one.

I care about Healthcare and Clinical AI because my experience has shown me that strong models alone are not enough to improve care. In my work, I have seen how signal noise, inconsistent data formats, and hard-to-use clinical information can weaken the value of an AI system before it ever reaches a clinician. That is a big reason I am interested in tools that improve documentation, make clinical data more usable, and reduce unnecessary administrative burden in healthcare settings. I am especially interested in practical systems that help clinicians work more efficiently while still keeping human judgment at the center of care.

- Generative AI can help convert free-text clinical notes into structured documentation that supports analytics, billing, and downstream model development.
- It can provide retrieval-based summaries of patient history, clinical guidelines, or relevant literature inside existing workflows to support clinical decision-making.
- It can assist with preprocessing and anomaly flagging for biomedical signals such as EEG and ECG, helping improve the reliability of downstream diagnostic models while keeping clinicians in the loop.
