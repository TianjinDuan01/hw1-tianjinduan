## Why I Care About Healthcare and Clinical AI

My direct experience processing EEG and ECG signals for diagnostic modeling has made concrete something that can otherwise seem abstract: the distance between raw clinical data and a decision that affects a patient is often a pipeline problem as much as a medical one. At Hopkins, I have seen how signal noise, inconsistent hardware configurations, and unstandardized schemas can degrade the reliability of ML outputs before a clinician ever sees a result. At Myya, converting unstructured clinical notes into structured datasets reinforced how much critical information remains trapped in formats that systems cannot use. These experiences point me toward a specific problem: clinical AI is often limited not by the sophistication of the models, but by the quality, structure, and accessibility of the data feeding them. I care about closing that gap in ways that reduce the administrative and cognitive burden on clinicians and support more consistent, evidence-based decision-making at the point of care.

## Three Ways Generative AI Could Help in Healthcare and Clinical AI

- **Structured documentation from clinical notes:** Generative AI can parse and convert free-text clinical narratives into structured, coded outputs, reducing the time clinicians spend on documentation and improving downstream data quality for analytics, billing, and ML model training.
- **Clinical decision support through RAG-based retrieval:** Retrieval-augmented generation systems can surface relevant clinical guidelines, patient history, or literature evidence at the point of care, giving clinicians a targeted, context-aware second reference without requiring them to leave their workflow.
- **Signal preprocessing and anomaly flagging:** Generative and hybrid models can assist in denoising biomedical signals such as EEG and ECG, flagging likely artifacts or anomalies for human review, which strengthens the reliability of downstream diagnostic models while keeping a clinician in the loop.
